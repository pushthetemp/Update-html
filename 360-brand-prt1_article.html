360 is not a binary state. Meaning, it doesn't just involve things of a digital nature i.e. video, mixed reality, virtual reality etc. In this case, it refers to being immersed or completely captivated by where you are within that moment in time. A good movie has the power to do this to the point when you leave; you forget you’re on earth. That’s video. Retail stores can be 360 too, this is when you don't straight away realise the place you have gone to buy your milk is actually a store. Why? Because you passed two cows, a disco and a herd of goats on the way in. 360 is different, and captivates more emotions than your standard.

A 360 brand takes the principles from above and applies it to your company. Now, I have come up with a case study for you- don't say I never treat you. For this one, we'll look at giving HMV a 360 brad and to make sure this article doesn't run too long, it form a series where the first will mainly be covering the mission statement.

Opening, we start with what the company is about; what's their mission, macro and micro goals to get achieving said mission? What inspires them? What makes them laugh, cry, angry? What makes them unlike anyone else?

Note: That last one “What makes them unlike anyone else?” often catches people out, so I’ll help out super quickly. It revolves around your methodology or maybe the kind of team you have- boasting the smartest by far in your market, the most aggressive in terms of willingness to face uncertainty and be open-minded about things. You could go on and on, but neither of us have all day, so that's the sort of thing you look at when outlining this. Let's have a look at HMV's vision/mission and the customer.

HMV is the home of entertainment. (I think) Its vision lies in becoming the hub for new discovery.

Whenever you think of HMV, you associate flicking through their racks looking for music, DVD's etc.; it's one of the few stores you venture into without a focused intent, other than the goal to find something you'll like. It's a place for the individual as well as established communities.

It’s good to include you’re ideal customer within your vision, especially through the sheer fact without them; you have zero company, let alone brand. You want to know who are they, what do they like doing, who are their MVP's (who they admire the most), what are their spending habits? Do they venture in packs or in a group? Let’s pretend that we've conducted meticulous research here and we know it like the back of our hands.

It's not the best to rely on what you do base on pure demographics: age, race, education level etc. rather, base the majority off psychographics which involves the elements mentioned at the beginning of this segment (i.e. common goals). That way you don’t limit the potential people you can reach; instead include people developing more and more dynamic and non-corresponding lives. Some just love music in its entirety whereby you'll see them browsing the shelves for Hip-Hip one second, then the next, they’re picking up a vinyl by Metallica. If you were to plot these people on a graph it would simply be filled with anomalies.

Ask yourself why things are the way they are. The reason they may be going to such contrasting genres and artist in terms of sound may be more related to the purpose for the music, or their ideology. Maybe they love nostalgic music. So heck yeah, now I understand why they go to Wu-Tang-Klan then. They are individuals that want to experience things that they don't get online; the look, feel and atmosphere. They like bumping into someone followed by spontaneous conversation. The age, you get everyone from 4 year olds looking for their Mr Men mug, to 14 year-olds on a mission to get their latest poster for their room.

The point is HMV serves many. And what their customers are looking for is unique, different, something for the die-hard fans as well as the experimental. Where else do you go to find a Walking Dead doormat along with the Sound track to the series with the extra themed mug just for good measure? I would only ever think of one place. I guess you could phrase it as "Entering a world of worlds".

I think this very iconic store has forgotten what made them so iconic in the first place. Thankfully their new owner Doug Putman understand the importance of HMV’s regeneration/revival, as well as the revival or retail in general, giving the nod to looking at implementing new technologies and experiences, engaging more with their current community, as well as forming new and stronger relationships. Under the watchful eye of Doug, HVM could once again be able to play a track we can all sing along to. Tell the dog, his master has a new voice.
